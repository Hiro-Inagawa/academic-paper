# 13. Reviewer response

After submission, most papers receive reviewer comments and a decision: accept, minor revise, major revise, or reject. If revise, you write a response letter. This file covers the response-letter craft.

Response letters are high-leverage. A strong response can rescue a borderline paper. A weak response can sink a paper that should have been accepted.

---

## Response-letter structure

### Standard format

```
Dear [Editor name or "Associate Editor"],

Thank you for the opportunity to revise our manuscript. We address each
reviewer's comments below. Reviewer comments are in italics; our
responses follow in plain text. Changes to the manuscript are marked in
the revised version and summarized at the end of each response.

=== Reviewer 1 ===

Overall comment:
[Italicized reviewer text]

Response:
Thank you for [specific aspect]. We have addressed this by [specific change].
See revised manuscript p. X, §Y.

Comment 1.1:
[Italicized reviewer text]

Response:
[Response paragraph]

...

=== Reviewer 2 ===

[Same format]

=== Editor comments ===

[Same format]

Sincerely,
[Author name]
on behalf of the authors
```

### Variations

- Some journals require a table format (reviewer comment / response / page reference).
- Some require the full original comments verbatim, not summarized.
- Some ask for a summary paragraph at the start describing major changes.

Check the journal's revision guidelines.

---

## The three-part response

For each substantive comment, include:

### 1. Reviewer's comment (verbatim or close paraphrase)

Quote the comment exactly. Do not selectively summarize — reviewers check.

### 2. Response

State what you did, or why you disagree. Short. Concrete. Starts with action verb where possible.

- "We have added..." / "We have revised..." / "We have clarified..."
- "We agree and have..."
- "We respectfully disagree because..."

### 3. Pointer to change

State where in the revised manuscript the change appears. Page number, section number, or both.

- "See revised manuscript, §3.2, p. 8."
- "New Table 2 reports the effect sizes."

Reviewers time-box their re-reading. A response that points at the exact change is more likely to be accepted than one that leaves the reviewer hunting.

---

## Tone

### Default

Respectful, specific, professional. Assume good faith. Reviewers often misunderstand; that is a failure of clarity, not a reason to be defensive.

### Acknowledging valid criticism

> "The reviewer correctly notes that our original framing overclaimed. We have revised the abstract and Introduction to hedge appropriately."

### Disagreeing with criticism

> "We respectfully disagree. The reviewer's concern about sample size is addressed by our power analysis (§4.1), which shows adequate power for the reported effects. We have added a sentence clarifying this at the point the reviewer flagged (p. 5, new sentence)."

Never: "The reviewer is wrong." Always: "We respectfully disagree because [specific reason]."

### Hostile reviewers

Some reviewers are rude, dismissive, or factually incorrect. Never match the tone. The editor reads your response and the reviewer's comments together. Your professionalism earns trust.

Acknowledge the concern, address it substantively, do not engage with hostility.

---

## Common response moves

### "We have done X"

When you fully addressed the concern.

> "Comment 2.1: The Methods section lacks detail on participant recruitment.
> Response: We have expanded §3.1 to describe recruitment in full, including
> the email template, screening criteria, and timeline. See revised manuscript
> §3.1, pp. 6-7."

### "We have added X to clarify"

When the concern was a misunderstanding.

> "Comment 1.3: The paper does not distinguish between condition A and B.
> Response: The original manuscript did distinguish these in §4.2, but we
> agree the distinction could be clearer. We have added a definition table
> (new Table 1) and a paragraph introducing the distinction earlier, in §2.
> See revised manuscript §2, p. 4; Table 1, p. 5."

### "We respectfully disagree"

When the concern is misplaced.

> "Comment 3.2: The authors should use model X instead of model Y.
> Response: We respectfully disagree. Model X is inappropriate because [specific
> technical reason]. We have added a sentence in §3.3 explaining our choice,
> citing [relevant source]. See revised manuscript §3.3, p. 9."

Use sparingly. Most "disagreements" should be reframed as misunderstandings you can address through clarification.

### "This is out of scope"

When the reviewer asks for something outside the paper's stated scope.

> "Comment 2.5: The paper should also address Z.
> Response: Z is an important question but outside the scope of the current
> study, which focused on [specific scope]. We have added a sentence in
> Limitations (§7) noting Z as a natural direction for future work. See
> revised manuscript §7, p. 18."

Avoid invoking scope too often. Two or three legitimate scope responses are acceptable; more suggests the paper's scope is unclear.

### "We have already addressed this"

When the reviewer missed an existing section.

> "Comment 1.7: The Methods should describe power analysis.
> Response: Power analysis is already described in §4.1, p. 7, paragraph 2,
> beginning 'We calculated required sample size...' We have revised the
> opening of §4.1 to make this more visible. See revised manuscript §4.1, p. 7."

Be gracious. The reviewer missed it; the writing was unclear. Fix the visibility.

---

## What must go in the response letter

- Every reviewer comment addressed, even trivial ones.
- Specific pointers to changes (page or section).
- Explicit acknowledgment of valid criticism.
- Explanation, not excuse, for criticism you disagree with.
- Summary at the end describing the overall change to the manuscript.

---

## What must NOT go in the response letter

- Emotional language ("frustrated," "surprised," "confused").
- Attacks on reviewer competence or character.
- Long philosophical tangents.
- Changes made that the reviewer did not request (mention briefly, do not emphasize).
- Disagreements about the paper's value or novelty (the editor decides those, not you).

---

## The diff statement

At the end of each reviewer's section, summarize the manuscript change.

> Summary of changes for Reviewer 1: Added power analysis detail (§4.1), new
> Table 1 clarifying definitions (§2), revised abstract to hedge appropriately.

This gives the editor and reviewer a quick map.

---

## Major revision vs minor revision

### Minor revision

Expected: small changes. Do not rewrite the paper. Do not add major new analyses unless a reviewer specifically requested them.

### Major revision

Expected: substantial work. Reviewers anticipate the paper will change meaningfully. Address all major concerns. Do not use this as an opportunity to radically reframe the paper unless reviewers asked for that.

---

## Rejection with encouragement to resubmit

Some venues allow resubmission after rejection. The resubmitted paper is usually treated as a new submission but retains connection to the prior review.

- Address the original reviewer concerns in the new manuscript.
- Include a brief cover letter explaining what changed.
- Do not guarantee the same reviewers will see the paper again.

---

## Rejection without resubmission option

If rejected without invitation to resubmit, move on. Do not argue with the editor. Do not appeal unless you have evidence of a factual error in the review.

Consider:
- Submitting to a different venue, addressing the previous reviewers' concerns.
- Revising substantially before the next submission.
- Seeking feedback from a colleague before resubmission.

---

## Appeal letters (rare)

If you believe the review contains factual errors or the editor overlooked something critical:

- Appeal to the editor, not to reviewers.
- Be specific: "The reviewer states X; the manuscript actually says Y on p. Z."
- Be brief. One page or less.
- Accept the editor's final decision.

Appeals rarely succeed and can damage future relationships with the journal. Use only when clearly warranted.

---

## Response letter checklist

Before sending:

1. Every reviewer comment has a response?
2. Every response includes a specific pointer to the manuscript change?
3. Tone is respectful throughout?
4. Disagreements are framed as "we respectfully disagree because..."?
5. No emotional language?
6. Summary of changes at the end of each reviewer's section?
7. Cover letter (if required) brief and factual?
8. Revised manuscript reflects every change mentioned in the response?
9. Diff file or tracked changes included if required?

If any answer is no: fix before sending.

---

## When in doubt

- If uncertain whether to respond: respond.
- If uncertain whether to make a change: consider the change, but it is acceptable to disagree with clear reasoning.
- If uncertain whether the tone is right: read aloud. If it sounds defensive or hostile, rewrite.
- If uncertain whether a comment is substantive: treat it as substantive. A two-sentence response is fine; a dismissed comment is not.

The goal: the editor finishes your response letter thinking "these authors have addressed the concerns." Not "these authors pushed back on everything."
