# 05. Argument structure

Booth, Colomb, and Williams in *The Craft of Research* define academic argument as five linked elements: claim, reason, evidence, warrant, acknowledgment. Use this structure to stress-test any claim before writing it.

---

## The five elements

### Claim

The assertion you defend. The core of the argument.

- Must pass the "so what?" test. If a reader can respond "so what?" without loss, the claim is not worth defending.
- Specific, not vague. "Model X improves accuracy by 12%" not "Model X is better."
- Contestable. If no one could disagree, it is not a claim, it is a fact.

### Reason

Why the claim holds. The "because."

- States the logical foundation.
- Often connects to prior knowledge or established findings.
- Answers the question "why should I believe this claim?"

### Evidence

What supports the reason. Data, quotations, examples, prior findings.

- Empirical: measurements, observations, counts.
- Textual: direct quotations from analyzed material.
- Prior literature: citations to established findings.
- Formal: mathematical derivation, logical proof.

### Warrant

The logical bridge between evidence and claim. Often implicit; make explicit when the connection is not obvious to a reader outside the field.

Example:
- Claim: "The intervention was effective."
- Reason: "Because outcomes improved for the treatment group."
- Evidence: "Treatment group scored 4.2 points higher than control (Cohen's d = 0.8, p = .002)."
- Warrant: "A 0.8 standard deviation improvement exceeds established thresholds for practical significance in this domain (Cohen, 1988)."

Without the warrant, a reader might wonder whether a 4.2 point difference is meaningful.

### Acknowledgment

Explicit recognition of counterarguments, limitations, or alternative interpretations.

- Strengthens the paper (reviewers will find weaknesses; better to name them first).
- Distinguishes serious scholarship from advocacy.
- Required in position papers and theory papers; expected in empirical papers.

## Three research question types

Booth et al. identify three types of questions research can address. Know which type yours addresses; this shapes the contribution.

### Practical

"How do we solve X problem?"

- Output: intervention, system, design.
- Evaluation: does it work?
- Common in: applied cs, engineering, HCI systems papers.

### Conceptual

"Why or how does X work? What is X?"

- Output: explanation, model, theory.
- Evaluation: does it fit the evidence? Does it predict well?
- Common in: science, theory papers, some HCI empirical work.

### Significance

"What are the broader implications of X?"

- Output: interpretation, critique, framework.
- Evaluation: does it change how we understand Y?
- Common in: humanities, social science, position papers.

### Apply to your paper

What question does your paper address? If you cannot name one of these three types, the paper's purpose is unclear.

## The "so what?" test

For every claim, ask: if a reader responds "so what?" what is the answer?

Three levels:

1. **So what (narrow)?** Why should someone in the same subfield care?
2. **So what (broad)?** Why should someone in the broader field care?
3. **So what (outside the field)?** Why should someone outside the field care?

Most papers answer #1 well. Papers that answer #2 or #3 in the Introduction and Discussion land more widely.

A paper that cannot answer even #1 should not be submitted yet.

## Revision heuristics

When revising an argument, check each of the five elements.

### Claim check

- Can I state the claim in one sentence?
- Is the claim specific?
- Is the claim contestable?
- Does the claim pass "so what?"

### Reason check

- Does each reason answer why the claim holds?
- Can I trace a reason to each of the three question types (practical, conceptual, significance)?
- Do the reasons connect to each other?

### Evidence check

- Does each reason have supporting evidence?
- Is the evidence specific (numbers, quotes, citations)?
- Is the evidence load-bearing (would the claim fail without it)?

### Warrant check

- Between each evidence and claim, is the logical bridge clear?
- If a reader from outside the field read this, would they see the connection?
- If the warrant is field-specific, should it be named explicitly?

### Acknowledgment check

- Have I named the strongest counterargument?
- Have I stated what this evidence does not establish?
- Have I noted the limits of my inference?

## Applying the structure per section

### Introduction

The CARS moves map to argument elements:
- Move 1 (Territory) establishes context for the claim.
- Move 2 (Niche) identifies what is unresolved — the reason this paper's contribution is needed.
- Move 3 (Occupy) states the claim.
- Contribution bullets are claim-atoms.

### Related Work

For each category of prior work:
- What did prior work claim? (Their claim)
- What did prior work show? (Their evidence)
- What is the warrant between their evidence and claim?
- What does this paper add? (Positioning statement)

### Discussion

The heaviest argumentation happens here.
- State the claim (from Results).
- Provide the reason (why this finding matters).
- Connect to evidence (the Results data).
- Explain the warrant (why the evidence supports the claim in this domain).
- Acknowledge limits and counterinterpretations.

## Common argument failures

### Claim without reason

"Our method is effective." (Why? For whom? Compared to what?)

### Reason without evidence

"Our method is effective because it captures context better." (What evidence shows this?)

### Evidence without warrant

"Accuracy improved by 12%." (Is 12% a meaningful improvement? By what standard?)

### Warrant without acknowledgment

"Because the 12% improvement exceeds established thresholds, the method is effective in practice." (But is the improvement generalizable? Was the comparison fair?)

### Acknowledgment as afterthought

Limitations listed at the end of the paper without connection to specific claims. Better: hedge each claim at the point of claim, in addition to listing limitations.

---

## Argument structure checklist

Before delivering a section with a central claim:

1. Can I state the claim in one sentence?
2. Is the reason stated explicitly?
3. Is the evidence cited or presented?
4. Is the warrant explicit if the connection is not obvious?
5. Have I acknowledged the strongest counterargument or limitation?

If any answer is no, revise.
